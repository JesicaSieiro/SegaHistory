History of Sega

  1. 1946

    Irving Bromberg and his son, Martin Jerome Bromberg, partnered with James L. Humpert to manufacture and distribute slot machines and other coin-operated devices, primarily to U.S. personnel stationed across Asia after World War II. The partnership, based in Honolulu, was called Service Games, referring to military service.

  2. 1951

    The United States Congress passed the Gambling Devices Transportation Act of 1951, banning slot machines on military bases within the U.S. territory. This forced Service Games to explore other markets.

  3. 1952

    Bromley sent Richard Stewart, a Service Games salesman, and Raymond Lemaire, a mechanic, to Japan to expand sales on U.S. military reservations. This led to the creation of Service Games, Japan, and over the next few years, a network of factories was established across Southeast Asia to cater to American troops. Service Games also began selling machines in Europe, becoming a major player in the worldwide slot machine market.

  4. 1954

    The Korean War saw American businessman and former Air Force officer David Rosen stationed in Japan. He returned in 1954 to establish Rosen Enterprises, Inc., initially an art exporting business. Rosen's company found success importing coin-operated instant photo booths from America, catering to the Japanese need for photographic ID cards.

  5. 1960

    Service Games, Japan, was liquidated and became two separate entities: Nihon Goraku Bussan (distribution, trading as "Uta Matic, Inc.") and Nihon Kikai Seizo (manufacturing, trading as "Sega, Inc."). Nihon Goraku Bussan grew significantly during the 1960s, becoming one of Japan's largest jukebox distributors due to a deal with Rock-Ola. The company continued to use the "Sega" brand, releasing the first original product to bear the name, the Sega 1000 jukebox, in 1960.

  6. 1961

    The American arcade industry had declined due to stagnation, prompting Rosen Enterprises to produce its own electro-mechanical arcade games, with engineer Hisashi Suzuki leading development.

  7. 1965

    Rosen negotiated a merger with Nihon Goraku Bussan, creating Sega Enterprises, Ltd. ("Sega" from Nihon Goraku Bussan and "Enterprises" from Rosen).

  8. 1966

    The newly formed Sega's core business was still in the jukebox sector, but this changed with the release of the 1966 hit, Periscope.

  9. 1968

    Sega continued producing innovative electro-mechanical arcade games and opened game centers in Japan, such as the Golden Center Game Corner in Yokohama in 1968.

  10. 1969

    Gulf+Western acquired Sega on May 3, 1969, allowing Sega to enter new markets such as importing American-made pinball tables and securing rights to properties from Paramount Pictures, another Gulf+Western company.

  11. 1974

    Sega became an American company in 1974 after its first public stock offering, with Sega Enterprises, Inc. as the head office and Sega Enterprises Ltd. as a Japanese subsidiary. Sega opened a factory on the West Coast and created Sega Centers in the USA, developing clean, inoffensive arcades often located in shopping malls.

  12. 1978

    With the success of Atari's Pong, Sega branched into video game importing and development, partnering with Gremlin Industries in North America. Sega began to establish its name with games like Turbo and Zaxxon and distributing Frogger in the States. Gremlin was acquired by Sega in 1978.

  13. 1980

    The company started targeting home consumers, creating products for individual ownership. This shift did not come without challenges. Early growth in the sector was stunted in the U.S. after the infamous market crash, but Sega persisted.

  14. 1982

    The company becoming Sega Electronics in 1982.

    Sega planned to distribute the ColecoVision console in Japan, but the deal fell through.

  15. 1983

    Sega entered the market with its own console, the SG-1000, and the SC-3000 microcomputer in June 1983. Despite competition from the Nintendo Famicom, Sega's arcade heritage kept its efforts afloat. The SC-3000 was distributed in Australia, New Zealand, and parts of Europe, and Sega began a sizable business in the Japanese toy market with Robo Pitcher.

    Financial troubles at Gulf+Western led to Sega Electronics being sold to Bally Midway in 1983, ending Sega's North American product development.

  16. 1984

    David Rosen, Hayao Nakayama, and Isao Okawa of CSK Corporation bought Sega's Japanese arm from Gulf+Western, turning it into a CSK subsidiary. Once again, Sega became a Japanese company.

  17. 1986

    Sega's big break came with its response to the Nintendo Entertainment System in the U.S.: the Sega Master System. While its impact in the U.S. was muted by Tonka's launch in 1986, the console found unexpected success in Western Europe and Brazil, displacing previous industry leaders like Atari Corporation. Popular titles like Alex Kidd in Miracle World and Wonder Boy helped Sega become the second most popular home system globally.

  18. 1987

    In the arcades, Sega's Japanese R&D teams revolutionized the industry with titles like Hang-On, Space Harrier, OutRun, and After Burner, which were later adapted for the Master System. By 1987, Sega operated 40,000 coin-operated machines in 2,000 locations worldwide. The late 1980s also saw Sega reassert itself in the amusement operations sector with the Hi-Tech Land Sega and Hi-Tech Sega game center chains in Japan, as well as the Time-Out venues in the USA.

  19. 1988-1990

    The Sega Mega Drive, released in Japan in October 1988, arrived in North America and Europe in 1989 and 1990, respectively. Strong marketing, new company recruits like Tom Kalinske, and cutting-edge technology helped the Mega Drive outpace Nintendo in most of the world, creating one of the most memorable "console wars" of all time.

  20. 1990s

    By 1991, Sega was selling products in over 44 countries and employing 1,695 people, expanding despite Japan's bubble economy bursting. Sega's new mascot, Sonic the Hedgehog, played a significant role in the company's success, particularly in the American and European markets.

    In the arcades, Sega innovated with titles like Virtua Racing and Virtua Fighter, powered by the Model 1 board, and the Mega Visor Display used in the VR-1 attraction. Sega's network of facilities included Sega World and GiGO chains in Japan and GameWorks centers in the Americas. Sega's ambition to become the Japanese equivalent of Disney saw the launch of Amusement Theme Parks, with plans for 100 indoor theme parks by 2000. Flagship locations included Tokyo Joypolis, SegaWorld London, and Sega World Sydney.

    However, the Sega Mega-CD and Sega 32X's lukewarm receptions, internal politics, and competition from the PlayStation strained Sega's home consumer division. Despite successes in the arcades with the Model 2 and 3, attendance at Sega's amusement centers declined, and the supporting infrastructure failed. By the late 1990s, Sega faced continuous financial losses, closed hundreds of amusement centers, and placed its hopes in the Sega Dreamcast and Sega NAOMI arcade hardware. Although popular among fans, these products were not enough to reverse Sega's fortunes, leading to a planned merger with Bandai being pulled and the company downsizing significantly.

IMPORTANT PEOPLE IN THE HISTORY OF SEGA

David Rosen

Founder of Sega.

Early Life and Career

  • Military Service: Served in the United States Air Force during the Korean War from 1949 to 1952, spending most of his time in Japan
  • Rosen Enterprises: Founded Rosen Enterprises in 1954, initially specializing in importing automated photo booths for photographic ID cards in Japan.

Entry into the Entertainment Market:

  • Shooting Games: Rosen Enterprises found success by importing shooting games from the US, leading to the proliferation of dedicated arcades in Japanese cities.
  • Industry Influence: Competitors like Taito and Nihon Goraku Bussan also entered the market.

Formation of Sega Enterprises Ltd.:

  • Merger: In October 1965, Rosen Enterprises merged with Nihon Goraku Bussan to form Sega Enterprises Ltd.
  • Leadership: Rosen became CEO and president of the newly formed company.

Industry Contributions:

  • NAMA: Instrumental in forming the first Japanese game manufacturers' association, NAMA, and served as its first chairman.

Sega's Evolution:

  • Gulf+Western Era: To facilitate public trading, Sega was sold to Gulf+Western. Rosen remained president and CEO during this period and became a director of Gulf+Western-owned Paramount Pictures in February 1982.
  • US Arm Sale: Gulf+Western sold Sega Electronics to Bally Midway to cut costs, leading to Rosen's departure from Sega at the end of 1983. He transitioned into a consulting role.

Return to Sega:

  • CSK Acquisition: In March 1984, Rosen, Hayao Nakayama, and Isao Okawa of CSK. Corporation purchased the Japanese arm of Sega, making it a CSK subsidiary.
  • Leadership Roles: Okawa became chairman, Nakayama became president, and Rosen became co-president. In 1986, he was also appointed co-president of the newly formed Sega of America.

Hayao Nakayama

President of Sega in the 1980s and 1990s, responsible for the company's success in the console market.

Career Overview:

  • Early Career: Started at Esco Trading, an arcade distribution company.
  • Sega Acquisition: Joined Sega management when Sega acquired Esco Trading in 1979.
  • Sega Independence: Played a pivotal role in helping David Rosen and Isao Okawa reclaim Sega's shares from Gulf+Western, leading to Sega's independence in 1984.
  • Sega Presidency: Served as Sega's president from 1984 to 1999, overseeing the company's rise in both the arcade and home console markets.
  • JAMMA: Founding figure of the Japan Amusement Machine and Marketing Association (JAMMA), serving as one of its first vice presidents in 1981.

1960s-1970s: Importing and Manufacturing

  • Importing American Pinball Machines: Sega began importing pinball machines from American manufacturers such as Bally, Stern, and Williams in the late 1960s.
  • Developing Own Tables: Throughout the 1970s, Sega developed its own pinball tables for export, despite continuing to import machines.
  • End of Japanese Manufacturing: Sega of Japan ceased manufacturing pinball tables in 1978.

1980s: Segasa and the Video Game Crash

  • Segasa (Sonic): The Spanish division of Sega, known as Segasa (later "Sonic" for the English market), continued to manufacture pinball machines until 1986.
  • 1983 Video Game Crash: Following the crash, Gulf+Western sold Sega's American assets to Bally in 1983. A year later, CSK purchased these assets from Bally.

1995-1999: Data East Acquisition and Sega Pinball

  • Acquisition of Data East: In 1995, Sega acquired the pinball division of Data East, which had previously belonged to Stern Electronics.
  • Pinball Table Production: From 1995 to 1999, Sega produced over 20 pinball tables featuring popular franchises like "Baywatch," "The X-Files," and "Mary Shelley's Frankenstein."
  • Popularity vs. Sales: Despite the tables' popularity in arcades, sales were relatively poor due to the declining pinball market.

1999: Transition to Stern Pinball

  • Sale to Gary Stern: In 1999, Sega sold its pinball division to Gary Stern, then-President of Sega Pinball. He established Stern Pinball, reviving the name connected to the original Stern Electronics sold to Data East in 1985, completing a full circle in the industry's history.

Early 1960s: Sega's Entry into EM Games

  • Early 1960s: Sega begins producing electro-mechanical arcade games, marking its entry into the amusement industry.
  • 1966: Release of "Periscope," Sega's first successful EM game, which establishes Sega's reputation in the EM market.

Late 1960s: Transition to Video Projection Games

  • Late 1960s: The emergence of "video projection" EM games, which use projected animated images instead of physical moving targets.
  • 1968: Sega releases "Duck Hunt," one of the first video projection EM games, setting the stage for future developments.

1970s: Decline of EM Games and Rise of Video Games

  • Early to Mid-1970s: Sega continues to produce EM games but begins to shift focus due to the growing reliability and popularity of video games.
  • 1976: Release of "Plinker's Canyon," marking the end of Sega's significant involvement in the EM game market.

Late 1970s: Transition to Video Games

  • 1978: The release of "Space Invaders" marks the beginning of the dominance of video games, leading to the decline of EM arcade games.

1980s: End of the EM Game Era

  • Early 1980s: Due to high maintenance costs and the rise of video games, EM arcade games become rare, with most being retired from service.

Before the introduction of Sega's System 1 arcade platform, Sega developed a variety of arcade systems, each with unique architectures and capabilities. These early systems were diverse, producing a wide array of game types, including vector-based, dual screen, and laserdisc titles. The acquisition of Gremlin Industries provided Sega with access to innovative technologies, which played a crucial role in the development of these arcade systems.

Notable Early Sega Arcade Systems and Games

Vector-Based Systems:

  • Eliminator (1981):
      • A multi-directional shooter that featured multiplayer gameplay and was notable for its vector graphics.
  • Star Trek (1982):
    • A vector-based arcade game where players controlled the U.S.S. Enterprise in various space combat missions.

Dual Screen Systems:

  • Deep Scan (1979):
    • A dual-screen game that combined elements of submarine warfare and sea battle gameplay.
  • Head On (1979):
    • An early maze game where players collected dots while avoiding collisions with other vehicles on a dual screen.

Laserdisc Systems:

  • GP World (1984):
    • A laserdisc-based racing game that featured realistic graphics and smooth animations thanks to its use of laserdisc technology.
    • Astron Belt (1983):
      • One of the first laserdisc arcade games, blending traditional video game mechanics with pre-recorded video footage for enhanced graphics and immersive gameplay.

Impact of Gremlin Industries Acquisition

Sega's acquisition of Gremlin Industries in 1978 was a pivotal moment in the development of these early arcade systems. Gremlin Industries brought in advanced technological expertise and innovations that enabled Sega to experiment with and develop a wide array of gaming experiences, from vector graphics to laserdisc technology. This acquisition allowed Sega to establish a strong foothold in the arcade market and laid the groundwork for their future successes with more advanced systems like the System 1.

The Sega System series marks the first set of consistent arcade hardware developed by Sega, following an experimental period in the late 1970s and early 1980s. This series primarily spanned from 1983 to 1992 and was succeeded by the Sega Model series. The defining characteristic of the Sega System hardware was the use of an 8-bit Zilog Z80 chip, initially as the main CPU and later for sound handling.

The Sega System series showcased Sega's innovation and evolution in arcade hardware, setting the stage for their later success in the arcade and home console markets.

Following the pioneering efforts of the Sega System series in two-dimensional gaming, the Sega Model series led the way in three-dimensional video game technology throughout most of the 1990s. This era marked a significant transition in arcade gaming as arcades faced competition from increasingly sophisticated home consoles. Sega's Model series hardware was instrumental in pushing the boundaries of arcade gaming, providing an immersive experience that home systems couldn't match at the time.

Key Developments and Games

Virtua Racing (1992): Marked Sega's foray into 3D racing games, showcasing the potential of 3D graphics in arcades.

Virtua Fighter (1993): Revolutionized the fighting game genre with its fully 3D polygonal characters and environments, setting a new standard for arcade fighting games.

Daytona USA (1994): Became one of the most successful arcade racing games, known for its advanced graphics, multiplayer capability, and enduring popularity.

Sega Rally Championship (1995): Introduced realistic rally racing to arcades, with attention to terrain and vehicle handling.

SG-1000

This is the first gaming system that Sega dedicated for home consumers. It was only released in Asia and Oceania, having enough sales to convincing Sega to continue developing video games for home. It had a white/grey simplistic design, with a top-loading cartridge and one wired controller. However, it was able to handle a second controller connected by a DE-9 port.

Controllers:

  • The first controller (model SJ-200) is hard-wired into the unit, making it difficult to replace.
  • A second controller can be connected via a DE-9 port on the right side of the console
  • Sega later released an adapter cable (JC-100) that allowed the use of standard detachable controllers, effectively replacing the hard-wired joystick.

Internal Hardware:

  • The SG-1000 shares a largely identical hardware setup with the ColecoVision, but it features a faster VRAM chip made by Fujitsu.
  • These hardware similarities explain the compatibility between the Telegames Personal Arcade, ColecoVision, and SG-1000, and also facilitate the unofficial porting of SG-1000 games to the ColecoVision.

SC-3000 Comparison:

  • The SC-3000, the computer counterpart to the SG-1000, shares the same hardware but includes more program RAM.
  • Both the SG-1000 and SC-3000 are comparable to the original MSX standard, which also has more program RAM.
  • Several MSX games have been ported to the SG-1000 in Taiwan and South Korea due to this hardware similarity.

Design and Usability:

  • Despite its appearance, the SG-1000 is a much flatter console than expected, with its unique shape and aesthetics giving the impression of a taller system.
  • The SJ-200 controller is often criticized for being stiff and unresponsive, and if it breaks, replacing it is challenging.
  • Future Sega consoles addressed this issue by allowing both controllers to be removable.
  • The SK-1100 keyboard can be plugged into the back of the SG-1000, enhancing its capabilities and aligning it more closely with the SC-3000.

Comparative Power:

  • The SG-1000 and SC-3000 are roughly on par with the original MSX standard and comparable to the Commodore 64 in terms of hardware capabilities.
  • However, they are not as powerful as Nintendo's Famicom/NES, which was a significant competitor during that era.

SEGA MASTER SYSTEM

Sega was going through a big pressure at the time, especially with products like the MSX or Nintendos Family Computer. Likewise, they managed to release the Sega Master System, a big improvement compared to its previous console. Anyway, sales didnt go as well as expected due to Nintendos dominance of Japanese market. The Master System is essentially a rebadged Sega Mark III and shares most of the same traits as Sega's earlier console. Like the Mark III and SG-1000, it is designed to play video games distributed on ROM cartridges (or Sega Cards) through a compatible television.

Compatibility:

  • The hardware builds on the design of the SG-1000, making it completely backwards compatible with the older console.
  • While technically capable of a degree of SC-3000 support, no versions of the Master System were ever designed with the ability to expand into a home computer.

Technical Specifications:

  • CPU: The Master System uses a Zilog Z80 CPU, which has an 8-bit data bus with both 8-bit and 16-bit registers.
  • Graphics Processor (VDP): The system features an 8/16-bit graphics processor with a 16-bit data bus and both 8-bit and 16-bit registers.
  • This combination of an 8-bit CPU with a 16-bit graphics processor was innovative and a precursor to the PC Engine (TurboGrafx-16), a more powerful console that was released in 1987 and began the 16-bit era, leading to the creation of the Sega Mega Drive (Genesis).

Media Format:

  • The Master System uses ROM cartridges and Sega Cards to distribute games. These media formats allowed for relatively easy and quick game distribution and swapping.

Backward Compatibility:

  • The Master System's design allows it to play games from the SG-1000, showcasing Sega's commitment to supporting their previous hardware and game library.

Hybrid Design:

  • The Master System's hybrid 8/16-bit architecture set a new standard in the gaming industry, paving the way for the next generation of consoles. This design approach allowed Sega to provide more advanced graphics and gameplay experiences compared to purely 8-bit systems.

SEGA MEGA DRIVE

The Mega Drive development started pretty soon after the release of the Master System. As usual, they built the console on previous existing hardware for reducing the manufacturing cost and make the software backwards compatible. The Mark V design was focused on efficiency.

Technological Advancements:

  • CPU: The Mega Drive uses the Motorola 68000, a 16-bit CPU that was already popular in arcade machines and home computers by the mid-1980s. This choice was intended to deliver superior performance and capabilities compared to the 8-bit systems.
  • Backward Compatibility: The Mega Drive builds on the technology found in the Master System and, with the use of adaptors, is fully backward compatible, allowing users to play their existing library of Master System games.

Key Features and Innovations:

  • Control Pad: The Mega Drive introduced an ergonomically designed control pad with three face buttons (A, B, and C), an improvement over the two-button controllers of previous consoles.
  • Stereo Sound: Unlike its competitors, the Mega Drive outputs sound in stereo, enhancing the audio experience for players.
  • Region Locking: The console makes an attempt to region-lock games through software, a feature aimed at controlling game distribution and compatibility across different regions.
  • Visual Output: With the right cables, the Mega Drive can produce a clearer image than its rivals, taking advantage of its higher resolution 320x240 display.

Design and Usability:

  • Top-Loading Cartridge Design: The Mega Drive features a top-loading design, which was more convenient and user-friendly compared to the VCR-style cartridge loading of the Western NES.
  • Removable Controllers: Unlike the Famicom, the Mega Drive's controllers are removable, providing greater flexibility and ease of use.
  • Hardware Expansion: The Mega Drive was designed from the beginning to support hardware expansions, allowing for future upgrades and additional functionality.
  • Durability: The console's use of dark plastic minimizes the issue of "yellowing" over time, which was common with older systems due to bromine-based flame retardants reacting with oxygen.

SEGA GAME GEAR

It was released in 1990 as a response to Nintendos Game Boy. It is Segas first attempt to compete in the handheld games market. Sega tried to improve and solve some hardware problems of the Game Boy for the Game Gear, as for example, adjusting the design for greater hand comfort or applying a colour LCD screen.

Design and Usability:

  • Form Factor: The Game Gear is held lengthwise at the sides, a design choice intended to prevent hand cramping, which was a common issue with the Game Boy.
  • Screen: It features a backlit, color LCD screen, providing clearer and more vibrant visuals than its main rival, the Game Boy.

Technical Specifications:

  • Derived from Master System: The Game Gear is largely derived from the Sega Master System. The primary differences include:
    • VDP (Video Display Processor): Capable of displaying palettes with a wider variety of colors.
    • Stereo Sound: Offers stereo sound playback, enhancing the audio experience.
    • Resolution: While Game Gear games typically run at a smaller resolution, the device is capable of playing games in higher resolutions due to its screen design.

Controls:

  • Buttons: The Game Gear features a D-Pad and two primary buttons (1 and 2), similar to the Master System, and adds a third button, START. This is one button short of the Game Boy's control scheme.

Power and Portability:

  • Batteries: The Game Gear requires six AA batteries for portable use. The backlit LCD screen, however, consumes a lot of power, leading to a battery life of only three to five hours. A battery pack is available to extend playtime.
  • Heat: The system generates more heat than the Game Boy, often causing "sweaty palms" after prolonged use.
  • Size: The Game Gear is bulkier than the Game Boy, making it less portable as it does not fit easily in many pockets.
  • AC Adaptor: An AC adaptor can be used to power the Game Gear from a wall outlet, though this was not a practical solution for most consumers.

Durability Issues:

  • Capacitor Problems: Game Gears were manufactured during a period when capacitor problems were common in the electronics industry. As a result, screen and audio failures are frequent, and repairs are often complicated.

SEGA MEGA-CD

At this moment, Sega was intending to release add-ons to the Mega Drive. They finally decided to make a CD-Rom upgrade to the Mega Drive called Mega-CD. It was intended to have 2-4 megabits of RAM, but it finally ended up receiving 6 Mb. The development of MEGA-CD was a closely guarded secret, but it finally was revealed at the Tokyo Toy Show 1991. Sega was not the only company to talk about CD-ROM-based consoles that day.

Design and Connectivity:

  • CD Reading Capability: The primary function of the Mega-CD was to read compact discs, which were becoming an increasingly affordable storage option in the late 1980s and early 1990s.
  • Connection to Mega Drive: The Mega-CD needs to be connected to a Mega Drive console via the expansion port located on the right-hand side of the Mega Drive.
  • Power Requirements: The Mega-CD requires its own AC adapter, meaning that both the Mega Drive and the Mega-CD need separate power sources, occupying two power sockets.

Hardware Enhancements:

  • Additional Processors and Memory: The Mega-CD includes extra processors, memory, and audio features that enhance the capabilities of the Mega Drive, but these enhancements can only be utilized by software specifically designed for the Mega-CD.
  • Graphics Capabilities: While the Mega-CD adds the ability to scale and rotate sprites, it does not significantly enhance the overall graphical capabilities of the Mega Drive.

Audio Features:

  • Stereo Sound: The Mega-CD provides stereo sound through RCA connectors. While the Mega Drive outputs mono sound to the television, stereo sound is available through the headphone port on the front of the console. By connecting the Mega Drive to the Mega-CD, all games can play in stereo through the television.

Additional Functions:

  • Compatibility with Sega 32X: The Mega-CD can be used with the Sega 32X add-on to play the six available Sega Mega-CD 32X games.
  • CD Playback: The Mega-CD can play regular 5-inch or 3-inch audio CDs and CD+G discs. It can function as a CD player without a television, as the audio can be controlled using button combinations on the Mega Drive.

SEGA 32X

Sega Mega-CD performed worst as intended, so they quickly had to recover the lead that they were winning thanks to the Mega Drive. The SEGA 32X was primarily envisioned as a system which would extend the SEGA Mega Drive and provide a slow grow of the Sega Saturn. The 32X didnt go well in Japan due to the awkward release schedule. Even so, the console had a great start in Europe, earning more than one million orders, but not enough were produced.

Design and Connectivity:

  • Physical Design: The 32X is a large, heavy, "mushroom-shaped" unit that plugs into the Mega Drive's cartridge slot. It comes with a plastic spacer to ensure a snug fit in most versions of the Mega Drive console.
  • Dual Functionality: The 32X acts as both a means to play specialized 32X cartridges and a passthrough device allowing standard Mega Drive games to be played.
  • Dependencies: Like the Mega-CD, the 32X cannot function as an independent unit and requires a powered Mega Drive to operate. It also requires its own AC adapter, and an additional connection to the Mega Drive console via a unique 32X Connector Cable

Power and Connections:

  • Multiple Power Adapters: If a user has both a 32X and a Mega-CD connected to their Mega Drive, they will need three separate power adapters, plus one for the television, making a total of four power adapters.
  • Unique Cables: The 32X uses a bespoke AC adapter similar to the Mega Drive 2 adapter and a unique connector cable, which was not sold separately but has third-party variants available.
  • Sega Power Strip: To address the issue of bulky adapters obstructing surrounding sockets, Sega released a dedicated Sega Power Strip in North America.

Hardware Enhancements:

  • Visual Upgrades: The 32X includes a VDP (Video Display Processor) that allows the Mega Drive to display up to 32,768 colors on-screen, significantly more than the usual 64 colors in standard Mega Drive titles. It also enhances 2D sprite rendering and supports 2D scaling, rotation, and texture-mapped 3D graphics.
  • Processors: The 32X adds two Hitachi SH-2 32-bit RISC processors, the same type used in the Sega Saturn, to handle the additional graphical and computational demands.
  • Audio Enhancements: The 32X includes a PWM audio chip for extra sound channels and QSound technology, which enables multidimensional sound similar to binaural recording. This enhances the audio experience by approximating 3D sounds through a regular stereo setup.

Compatibility and Limitations:

  • Sega Mega-CD Compatibility: The 32X is compatible with the Sega Mega-CD, allowing users to play enhanced Sega Mega-CD 32X games. This combination enables higher quality full motion video (FMV) with a wider color palette than standard Mega-CD games.
  • Region Locking: The 32X does not have regional lockout technology but relies on the Mega Drive's region to determine compatibility. It uses a Genlock frequency to differentiate between 50Hz (PAL) and 60Hz (NTSC) games, which means Japanese and Genesis 32X units are identical and can work on any NTSC console. Region modifications can be easily made for universal game support.

MOST POPULAR GAMES OF THE TIME

SG-1000

Monaco-GP

Ported from the arcade version released in 1979, the main objective is to horizontally maneuver a vehicle while avoiding obstacles and other cars. The player has 3 lives and a score that increases with the distance traveled.

SEGA Galaga

The player has three lives and controls a ship that must defeat aliens with different movement patterns. Once eliminated, they will award points and allow progression to the next level.

Star Force

Vertical scrolling shoot 'em up. Like other games in the genre, the player starts with 3 lives, which are lost upon contact with enemies.

Doki Doki Penguin Land

Platformer game where the player must solve puzzles with various blocks and enemies. It features vertical scrolling, descending to progress through levels.

Girls Garden

Action title where the player must collect flowers while avoiding bears. After collecting all the flowers, they must be delivered to advance to the next level. Finally, every two levels, a "bonus or extra" stage will appear.

Choplifter

It is a side-scrolling action game that was originally released for the Apple II computer. The Player controls a combat helicopter that must retrieve prisoners and then transport them back to players home base.

Space Invaders

The first shooter game and the progenitor of the shoot em up genre. It was ported to SG-1000 in 1985.

Master System

Alex Kidd

An 8-bit platformer video game featuring Alex Kidd. It includes vertical and horizontal scrolling and various mechanics such as enemies, block breaking, and shops. Alex Kidd was Sega's first iconic character and served as the company's "mascot."

Fantasy Zone

Scrolling shooter released in arcades by SEGA, later ported to the Master System. The player controls a ship that must defeat various enemies in each level, ultimately facing the boss.

Phantasy Star

It is a RPG released in 1987, considered one of the first games of this genre for consoles. It alters between a top-down perspective when the player is exploring the overworld, and changes to a first-person view when engaged in a battle or exploring dungeons.

Golden Axe Warriors

An action-adventure RPG released in 1991. It is a spinoff of the Golden Axe series. Ultimately, the game was not well received by the public due to its differences from the original title in the series.

California Games

This game consists of a collection of outdoor sports that are popular in California. The gameplay is based on different minigames, each one with unique sprites and stages.

Ecco the Dolphin

It involves a dolphin that the player must control to navigate through mazes and horizontal levels. The game is set in a science fiction universe featuring elements like space, aliens, and other races.

Mortal Kombat

The first title of the series was released in 1992, capitalizing on the fighting game craze ignited by Street Fighter II. It was the first game to employ photorealistic graphics captured with a camera. It was ported to the Master System, as well as other consoles.

MEGA DRIVE

Sonic the Hedgehog

The first game of the iconic hedgehog saga, released in 1991. It's a horizontal scrolling platformer where the player navigates through various levels filled with enemies and obstacles. Sonic became Sega's new mascot, becoming an emblematic element for the company.

Street of Rage

A 16-bit beat 'em up game released by SEGA AM7. Players must defeat enemies to obtain weapons, power-ups, or money in horizontal scrolling levels. The game follows three police officers trying to free themselves from a mafia gang.

Eternal Champions

A fighting game developed exclusively for the Mega Drive by Sega, featuring 1 vs 1 battles determined by the players' life bars. The title includes Fatalities, a feature popularized by Mortal Kombat.

The Revenge of Shinob

The second game in the Shinobi series, developed by SEGA. The player controls Joe, who must try to save Naoko from Neo Zeed. It is a platformer with horizontal scrolling levels that features a wide variety of objects and enemies.

Street of Rage II

A horizontal scrolling beat 'em up where the player must defeat various enemies to progress through different stages. The player needs to manage their life bar, displayed in the top left corner of the screen, as well as various attacks and weapons dropped by enemies. Four fighters must rescue Adam from Mr. X and his criminal organization.

Castlevania Bloodlines

John and Eric, two vampire hunters, embark on an adventure across Europe to eliminate the threat of Dracula, who was previously defeated. However, Elizabeth is trying to revive him. The basic idea of the game is to progress through levels, collecting gems to obtain better equipment.

Monster World IV

This title is a mix of several genres, including action, platformer, and RPG. The game was released exclusively in Japan in 1994 and follows Asha, a girl who aims to help spirits in danger.

GAME GEAR

Columns

A puzzle game developed by SEGA to compete with the famous Tetris. The title features rows of different colored blocks that fall down the screen, which the player can rotate or move horizontally. If a piece connects with two or more blocks of the same color, they will disappear and award points.

Sonic Chaos

In this game featuring the iconic hedgehog, Sonic is accompanied by Tails the Fox on a mission to recover the Chaos Emeralds. Like other titles in the series, it is a 2D platform adventure where the player must progress through levels.

Defenders of Oasis

An RPG based on One Thousand and One Nights where the player controls the Prince of Shanadar, who must defeat the empire that has attacked his kingdom. When the player encounters an enemy, the game enters a turn-based combat phase.

Land of Illusion Starring Mickey Mouse

Platform game developed for Game Gear and Master System that contains 14 stages with sidescrolling. Mickey can defeat enemies jumping on them or picking up an item and then throwing it.

Mega Man

The first game of the well-known Mega Man saga to reach the Game Gear. It is a complete new game that takes inspiration and different elements from Mega Man 2, 4 and 5. It is an action-platformer game in which the player will have to complete different stages for defeating the main four bosses.

Master of Darkness

It is a platform game with some similar aspects with Castlevania. While advancing through the levels, the player will have to defeat enemies with a variety of primary and secondary weapons. The main objective is to defeat Count Dracula, who is guilty of a series of murders.

MEGA-CD

Sonic CD

This title of the Sonic saga maintains all the elements of the previous ones, including a new time travel mechanic. This allows access to different versions of each stage, with completely new designs, music and graphics. It also introduce two new characters, Metal Sonic and Amy Rose.

Final Fight

Beat-em Up developed by Capcom, it is the first game of the saga. The player is set in Metro City, a fictitious city in the United States, where they will have to rescue Jessica, Mike Haggars daughter from Damnd, the leader of a gang.

Shining Force CD

It is a recreation of Shining Force Gaiden and Shining Force: The Sword of Hajya from the Game Gear. The game is divided into 4 stories, the two first of them being adapted versions of those of the Game Gear. The gameplay remains faithful to the previous titles, being a tactical RPG where the progress of the characters is obtained by defeating enemies in their respective battle.

The Terminator

Game released in 1993 based on the 1984 movie The Terminator. It is a platform shootem up that includes full motion video from the movie. The game was really well received due to its graphics and soundtrack

Night Trap

It is an interactive film mainly composed of full-motion video, recorded with VHS tape technology. The player will have to prevent the girls from being trapped by the vampires trying to enter the house.
